# Maqasid Al-Shariah

The higher objectives of Islamic law (Shariah), articulated by classical scholars such as Al-Ghazali and Al-Shatibi, and modernized by Jasser Auda as a systems approach to Islamic jurisprudence. The framework identifies the fundamental purposes that Shariah seeks to protect and promote, serving as the evaluative lens for all design decisions in [[maqasid-os]] -- prioritizing purposefulness over literalism.

## Core Idea

Maqasid al-Shariah represents a shift from reductionist literalism to holistic, purpose-oriented legal philosophy. The law is understood not as a collection of isolated rulings but as an integrated system governed by higher objectives. Validity of legal reasoning (ijtihad) is ultimately determined by its ability to realize these objectives.

### The Five Higher Objectives (al-Daruriyyat al-Khams)

The classical formulation identifies five essential objectives:

1. **Faith (din)** -- Hifz al-Din
2. **The Soul / Life (nafs)** -- Hifz al-Nafs
3. **The Mind / Intellect ('aql)** -- Hifz al-Aql
4. **Offspring / Family (nasl)** -- Hifz al-Nasl
5. **Wealth (mal)** -- Hifz al-Mal

Contemporary scholars have expanded the operative verb from mere "protection" (hifz) to "development and rights" -- a shift from defensive preservation to active cultivation. Modern additions to the discourse include human dignity, freedom of belief, and care for family as first-class objectives. Jasser Auda proposes "Human Development" as a standalone objective, measurable by empirical standards such as the UN Human Development Index.

### The Seven Pillars in Maqasid OS

In [[maqasid-os]], the classical five are extended to seven pillars, adding Environment (bi'ah) and Ummah (community) to reflect a holistic view of human flourishing. These seven pillars form the information architecture of the entire system:

1. **Faith** -- Hifz al-Din
2. **Life** -- Hifz al-Nafs
3. **Intellect** -- Hifz al-Aql
4. **Family** -- Hifz al-Nasl
5. **Wealth** -- Hifz al-Mal
6. **Environment** -- Hifz al-Bi'ah
7. **Ummah** -- Hifz al-Ummah

## Jasser Auda's Six Systems Features

Auda reframes the Maqasid not as a static hierarchy but as an interconnected system. His six systems features provide the theoretical foundation for treating the Maqasid as a living architecture in [[maqasid-os]]:

1. **Cognitive Nature** -- Fiqh is human cognition of revelation, not revelation itself. This validates pluralism in legal interpretation; multiple valid readings can coexist.
2. **Wholeness** -- Holistic evidence and universal principles take precedence over partial, isolated rulings. The system is more than the sum of its parts.
3. **Openness** -- The law operates as an open system that interacts with its environment. It is open to insights from natural sciences, social sciences, and changing contexts.
4. **Interrelated Hierarchy** -- Moves beyond binary classifications (obligatory/forbidden) to recognize interrelationships between legal concepts at multiple levels.
5. **Multi-dimensionality** -- Replaces binary certain/uncertain logic with a spectrum of probability, allowing nuanced reasoning.
6. **Purposefulness** (the core feature) -- The validity of ijtihad is determined by its ability to realize the higher objectives. Purpose is the organizing principle of the entire system.

### Software and Systems Mapping

Auda's systems lens maps directly onto software architecture concepts:

- **Equifinality** -- A system can reach the same objective through various valid means depending on the environment. Multiple implementations can serve the same Maqsad.
- **Program adaptability** -- Rulings (the program) change with inputs (social and historical context) to keep the output (justice and welfare) constant. The invariant is the objective, not the method.
- **Self-renewal (autopoiesis)** -- The system renews itself through philosophical openness, absorbing new knowledge without losing its identity.

## Ethical Screening

The Maqasid framework moves ethical evaluation from formal procedural compliance to moral outcomes:

- Any ruling that replaces justice with injustice does not belong to Shariah -- purpose overrides form.
- "Implication of purpose" (dilalat al-maqsad) screens actions against their actual results, not just their procedural validity.
- The [[covenant-architecture]] draws from the Scroll of Madinah (Sahifat al-Madinah) -- a model of peaceful co-existence, pluralism, and mutual obligation.
- The [[amanah-gate]] operationalizes this screening at the task level: if a task undermines any of the seven objectives, it is flagged before execution.

## Application in Maqasid OS

- Each pillar has its own **dashboard** in [[maqasid-os]] with three-tier detail design (overview, module, detail).
- **Readiness checks** grounded in Quranic ayat assess the user's alignment with each pillar before task execution. See [[ceremony-gate-pattern]].
- **Seed tasks** are generated per pillar, giving users a starting point rooted in the objective that pillar protects.
- The [[amanah-gate]] uses the Maqasid as the evaluative framework for permissibility.
- The [[bbos-pipeline]] grounds its stages in divine names and maps business operations back to these objectives.
- The [[covenant-architecture]] treats the seven pillars as the structural spine of the entire system.
